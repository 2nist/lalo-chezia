> Based on *Open Music Theory v2* — Gotham, Gullings, Hamm, Hughes, Jarvis, Lavengood, Peterson

## I. Fundamentals

### Notation Basics
- Notes are written on a **5-line staff** using **clefs** to define pitch range
  - **Treble clef** — higher range (guitar, voice, piano right hand)
  - **Bass clef** — lower range (bass guitar, piano left hand, cello)
  - **Alto/Tenor clef** — middle range (viola, trombone)
- **Ledger lines** extend the staff for very high or very low notes
- **ASPN (American Standard Pitch Notation):** C4 = Middle C (261.63 Hz), A4 = 440 Hz

### Half Steps, Whole Steps & Accidentals
- **Half step (semitone):** smallest interval in Western music — adjacent keys on piano
- **Whole step:** two half steps
- **Accidentals:** ♯ (sharp, raise ½ step), ♭ (flat, lower ½ step), ♮ (natural, cancel)
- **Enharmonic equivalence:** same pitch, different name (F♯ = G♭)

### Rhythmic Values
| Note | Symbol | Value in 4/4 |
|---|---|---|
| Whole note | 𝅝 | 4 beats |
| Half note | 𝅗𝅥 | 2 beats |
| Quarter note | ♩ | 1 beat |
| Eighth note | ♪ | ½ beat |
| Sixteenth note | ♬ | ¼ beat |

- **Dots** add half the note's value (dotted quarter = 1.5 beats)
- **Ties** extend a note's duration across beats/barlines
- **Rests** = silence of equivalent duration

## II. Meter & Time Signatures

### Simple Meter
Beat divides into **2** equal parts.

| Time Sig | Beats per Measure | Beat Note Value |
|---|---|---|
| 2/4 | 2 | Quarter note |
| 3/4 | 3 | Quarter note |
| **4/4** | **4** | **Quarter note** |
| 2/2 (cut time) | 2 | Half note |

**Counting 4/4:** 1 – and – 2 – and – 3 – and – 4 – and

### Compound Meter
Beat divides into **3** equal parts.

| Time Sig | Beats per Measure | Beat Note Value |
|---|---|---|
| **6/8** | 2 | Dotted quarter |
| 9/8 | 3 | Dotted quarter |
| 12/8 | 4 | Dotted quarter |

**Counting 6/8:** 1 – 2 – 3 – 2 – 2 – 3 (2 beats, each divided into 3)

### Other Rhythmic Essentials
- **Borrowed divisions (tuplets):** Dividing a beat into a number not native to the meter
  - Triplet in 4/4 = 3 notes in the space of 2
  - Duplet in 6/8 = 2 notes in the space of 3
- **Syncopation:** Accenting weak beats or off-beats
- **Hypermeter:** Meter at a higher level than the measure (phrases of 2, 4, or 8 bars)

## III. Scales & Keys

### Major Scale
**Formula:** W W H W W W H

All major scales follow this same pattern of whole and half steps from the root.

| Key | Notes |
|---|---|
| C Major | C D E F G A B C |
| G Major | G A B C D E F♯ G |
| F Major | F G A B♭ C D E F |

**Key Signatures** indicate which notes are consistently sharp or flat throughout a piece.

### Minor Scales
- **Natural Minor** (Aeolian): W H W W H W W
- **Harmonic Minor:** same as natural, but ♭7 raised to 7 (creates leading tone)
- **Melodic Minor:** ascending — raises ♭6 and ♭7; descending — reverts to natural minor

### Scale Degree Names
| Degree | Name |
|---|---|
| 1 | Tonic |
| 2 | Supertonic |
| 3 | Mediant |
| 4 | Subdominant |
| 5 | Dominant |
| 6 | Submediant |
| 7 | Leading Tone (major) / Subtonic (minor) |

### Circle of Fifths
- Moving clockwise: add 1 sharp per key (C → G → D → A → E → B → F♯)
- Moving counter-clockwise: add 1 flat per key (C → F → B♭ → E♭ → A♭ → D♭ → G♭)
- Keys directly across from each other are **tritone related**

### Diatonic Modes
Modes derived from the major scale by starting on a different degree:

| Mode | Degree | Formula Compared to Major |
|---|---|---|
| Ionian | 1 | = Major scale |
| Dorian | 2 | ♭3, ♭7 |
| Phrygian | 3 | ♭2, ♭3, ♭6, ♭7 |
| Lydian | 4 | ♯4 |
| Mixolydian | 5 | ♭7 |
| Aeolian | 6 | = Natural Minor |
| Locrian | 7 | ♭2, ♭3, ♭5, ♭6, ♭7 |

## IV. Intervals

### Interval Size (Generic)
Counted by letter names (including both notes): C to E = a **3rd** (C, D, E)

### Quality
| Size | Perfect | Major | Minor | Aug | Dim |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 2nd | — | 2 semitones | 1 semitone | 3 | 0 |
| 3rd | — | 4 | 3 | 5 | 2 |
| 4th | 5 | — | — | 6 | 4 |
| 5th | 7 | — | — | 8 | 6 |
| 6th | — | 9 | 8 | 10 | 7 |
| 7th | — | 11 | 10 | 12 | 9 |
| Octave | 12 | — | — | 13 | 11 |

### Consonance & Dissonance
- **Consonant:** unison, octave, perfect 5th, 3rds, 6ths — stable
- **Dissonant:** 2nds, 7ths, tritone — unstable, seek resolution

## V. Chords & Roman Numeral Analysis

### Triads
Built by stacking **thirds** above a root:

| Quality | Structure | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Major | M3 + m3 | C – E – G |
| Minor | m3 + M3 | C – E♭ – G |
| Diminished | m3 + m3 | C – E♭ – G♭ |
| Augmented | M3 + M3 | C – E – G♯ |

### Seventh Chords
| Name | Structure | Symbol |
|---|---|---|
| Major 7th | Major triad + M7 | Cmaj7 |
| Dominant 7th | Major triad + m7 | C7 |
| Minor 7th | Minor triad + m7 | Cm7 |
| Half-Diminished | Dim triad + m7 | Cø7 |
| Fully Diminished | Dim triad + dim7 | Cdim7 |

### Roman Numeral Analysis
- **Uppercase** numerals = major chords (I, IV, V)
- **Lowercase** numerals = minor chords (ii, iii, vi)
- Degree symbol (°) = diminished; + = augmented
- Figured bass symbols show inversions: 6 = first inversion, 6/4 = second inversion

### Inversion Notation
| Triad | Bass Note | Figure |
|---|---|---|
| Root position | Root | (none) |
| 1st inversion | 3rd | 6 or 6/3 |
| 2nd inversion | 5th | 6/4 |

## VI. Counterpoint & Voice Leading

### Types of Motion Between Two Voices
| Motion | Description |
|---|---|
| **Parallel** | Both voices move in same direction by same interval |
| **Similar** | Both voices move in same direction, different intervals |
| **Contrary** | Voices move in opposite directions |
| **Oblique** | One voice stays, other moves |

### Rules for Good Voice Leading
- Avoid **parallel 5ths** and **parallel octaves** (between any two voices)
- Prefer **contrary motion** to maintain independence
- Resolve **tendency tones**: leading tone (7) → tonic (1); ♭7 in Dorian → stays or descends
- Avoid large leaps (especially augmented intervals)

### Species Counterpoint (Introduction)
A systematic approach to learning melodic and harmonic writing:
1. **First Species:** note-against-note (consonances only)
2. **Second Species:** 2 notes per cantus note (passing tones allowed on weak beats)
3. **Third Species:** 4 notes per cantus note (more elaborate passing/neighbor tones)
4. **Fourth Species:** syncopated notes, suspensions
5. **Fifth Species:** free combination of all previous species

## VII. Form

### Phrase-Level Forms
- **Motive:** smallest recognizable melodic/rhythmic unit
- **Phrase:** a complete musical thought, typically 4–8 bars, ending in a cadence
- **Period:** two phrases — an antecedent (ends on half cadence) + consequent (ends on authentic cadence)
- **Sentence:** a phrase that begins with a basic idea, repeats it, then expands/concludes

### Larger Forms
| Form | Structure | Common In |
|---|---|---|
| **Binary (AB)** | Two contrasting sections | Baroque dances |
| **Ternary (ABA)** | Statement – contrast – return | Many styles |
| **Rounded Binary** | A then B that returns to A material | Baroque, Classical |
| **Sonata Form** | Exposition – Development – Recapitulation | Classical symphonies |
| **Rondo (ABACA)** | Recurring refrain alternates with episodes | Classical |
| **Through-composed** | No repeated sections | Art songs, film |

### Song Form (Popular Music)
| Section | Function |
|---|---|
| **Intro** | Establishes key, mood, groove |
| **Verse** | Narrative, usually lower energy |
| **Pre-chorus** | Builds tension toward chorus |
| **Chorus** | Hook, highest energy, repeated |
| **Bridge** | Contrast, new perspective |
| **Outro/Coda** | Winds down, final cadence or fade |

## VIII. Texture

| Texture | Description | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Monophony** | Single melodic line, no harmony | Gregorian chant |
| **Homophony** | Melody + supporting chords/accompaniment | Most pop songs |
| **Polyphony** | Multiple independent melodic lines | Bach fugues |
| **Heterophony** | Same melody performed simultaneously with variations | Folk traditions |

## IX. Galant Schemas (Common Melodic Patterns)

Recognizable melodic/harmonic patterns in 18th-century music — useful as building blocks:
- **Meyer:** ascending scale through tonic triad, common opening gesture
- **Prinner:** descending pattern, common closing/cadential gesture
- **Rule of the Octave:** standardized harmonization of a bass line ascending or descending through the scale
- **Sequences:** repeating harmonic/melodic patterns transposed by a fixed interval
